# 👋 Hi, I'm Jonathan Nelson

I am a Statistics graduate from Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology, Ghana, with a growing research focus in **statistical modelling, financial econometrics, commodity risk, machine learning and public-sector data systems**.

My work is mainly about one question:

> How can countries like Ghana use better data and stronger quantitative methods to understand economic and financial risk?

I am especially interested in Ghana’s exposure to **cocoa, gold, crude oil and the cedi**, and how these markets connect to exchange-rate pressure, inflation risk, export concentration and macro-financial vulnerability.

---

## 👀 What I am working on

I am currently building an independent research portfolio around emerging-market risk, with Ghana as my main case study.

### Commodity tail risk and extreme dependence

I studied whether cocoa, gold and crude oil become more dangerous together during crisis periods using copula and extreme-value methods.

The result was not the dramatic one I expected. After multiple-testing correction, I did not find robust evidence that Ghana’s major export commodities experience stronger crisis-driven tail dependence during stress periods.

That null result matters because in risk modelling, a careful “no” can be just as useful as a positive finding.

Repository:  
[tail-dependence-ghana](https://github.com/Jonathanerrils/tail-dependence-ghana)

---

### Machine learning versus statistical models for Ghanaian risk signals

I compared eleven machine learning and statistical models for commodity and currency risk signals in Ghana, using nested time-series cross-validation and formal significance testing.

One lesson from this project was simple:

> A model can be numerically ahead and still not be statistically better.

That changed the way I think about model selection, especially for small and noisy emerging-market datasets.

Repository:  
[robust-ml-vs-stats-ghana](https://github.com/Jonathanerrils/robust-ml-vs-stats-ghana)

---

### Bayesian stochastic volatility modelling

I am also putting together a third project on Bayesian stochastic volatility models for cocoa, gold and crude oil, benchmarked against GARCH-type and Ornstein–Uhlenbeck alternatives.

The aim is to study how volatility and tail risk behave in commodity-dependent economies, especially when the data are noisy and the shocks are large.

## 🌱 Background

I graduated with a BSc in Statistics from KNUST, where my strongest work was in applied time series, multivariate analysis and statistical modelling.

My final-year project compared ARIMA, Exponential Smoothing and Prophet for forecasting Ghana’s GDP using data from 1960 to 2023. That project pushed me from simply applying models to asking deeper questions about why a model works, what structure it captures and what the result actually means.

I have also worked as a Teaching and Research Assistant at KNUST, supported data collection and statistical analysis, and interned with the Ghana Statistical Service, where I gained exposure to regional economic and demographic data.

## 💞️ What I care about

I care about research that is:

- statistically honest;
- reproducible;
- useful for real decisions;
- clear enough for non-specialists to understand;
- grounded in the realities of African and emerging-market data.

I try not to treat models as black boxes or results as trophies. I am more interested in whether the evidence survives careful testing.

Sometimes that means reporting a null result.  
Sometimes it means rebuilding a data pipeline.  
Sometimes it means admitting that a model looked good until the validation exposed its weakness.

That is the kind of research discipline I want to keep developing.
